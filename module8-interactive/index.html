The AI Landscape 🌐
From Turing's dream to ChatGPT — trace the history of AI, understand the key milestones, and see how the field fits together in 2025.
📜 History 🗓️ Key Milestones 🌊 AI Winters 🚀 Modern AI
🌐
What is Artificial Intelligence?
The big picture before the details
The Core Idea
Artificial Intelligence is the science and engineering of creating machines that exhibit intelligent behaviour — perceiving the world, reasoning about it, learning from experience, and taking actions to achieve goals.

AI is not a single technology. It is an umbrella term covering dozens of sub-fields: machine learning, computer vision, natural language processing, robotics, knowledge representation, planning, and more.
🎯 Narrow AI (ANI)
Designed for one specific task. Almost all AI deployed today is narrow — a chess engine, spam filter, face recognition system, or recommendation algorithm. Excellent within its domain, but cannot transfer that skill.
🌌 General AI (AGI)
A hypothetical system with human-level reasoning across any task. No AGI exists yet. Researchers debate timelines vigorously — estimates range from "within a decade" to "never." This is the long-term goal many labs pursue.
💡Most practical AI work today — and everything on this course — involves Narrow AI. When the media talks about "AI taking over," they conflate narrow tools with sci-fi AGI.
📜
A Brief History of AI
From logic machines to large language models
1943–1950s · The Birth
Logical neurons & the Turing Test
McCulloch & Pitts publish the first mathematical model of a neuron (1943). Alan Turing proposes the "Imitation Game" (1950) as a test for machine intelligence. The term "Artificial Intelligence" is coined at the Dartmouth Conference 1956 by John McCarthy.
1960s–1970s · Early Optimism
Expert systems & first neural nets
Researchers build early rule-based programs: ELIZA (1966), a chatbot; DENDRAL (1965), a chemistry expert. Frank Rosenblatt invents the Perceptron — the first trainable neural network. Excitement is high but machines can't yet generalise.
1974–1980 · First AI Winter
Funding cuts & disillusionment
The Lighthill Report (UK) criticises AI progress. Minsky & Papert prove perceptrons can't solve simple problems like XOR. Government funding collapses. Researchers flee to other fields — the first AI winter.
1980s · Expert Systems Boom
Knowledge engineering & second winter
Rule-based expert systems like MYCIN (medical diagnosis) succeed commercially. Japan's "Fifth Generation" project spends billions. By 1987, the market collapses — maintaining rules is brittle and expensive. The second AI winter begins.
1990s–2000s · Statistical Revolution
ML replaces rules; data-driven AI emerges
Support Vector Machines, Random Forests, and Bayesian networks dominate. Deep Blue beats Kasparov at chess (1997). The internet creates vast datasets. Geoffrey Hinton revives neural networks with backpropagation research throughout the decade.
2012 · Deep Learning Breakthrough
AlexNet & the GPU revolution
Krizhevsky, Sutskever & Hinton's AlexNet crushes the ImageNet competition by 10+ percentage points using a deep convolutional neural network on GPUs. This single result reshapes the field. Deep learning becomes mainstream.
2017 · The Transformer
"Attention Is All You Need"
Google Brain publishes the Transformer architecture — the foundation of every modern LLM. Self-attention allows models to process entire sequences in parallel, enabling unprecedented scale. Within 2 years, BERT and GPT-2 follow.
2020–Present · Foundation Models
GPT-4, Gemini, Claude, Stable Diffusion & beyond
GPT-3 (2020, 175B params) shocks researchers with emergent capabilities. DALL-E, Stable Diffusion, Midjourney democratise image generation. ChatGPT (Nov 2022) reaches 100M users in 2 months. AI is now mainstream and transformative.
❄️Notice the two AI winters: periods of over-hype followed by funding collapse. Many argue we should learn from these cycles as AI excitement peaks again. Hype is real — but so is the underlying progress.

AI vs ML vs Deep Learning 🧠
Three buzzwords, one nested relationship. Understand what each term really means — and which problems each approach solves best.
🤖 AI📊 Machine Learning⚡ Deep Learning🔄 Comparison
🎯
The Nesting Relationship
Every DL model is an ML model. Every ML model is an AI system.
🤖 Artificial Intelligence
Definition: Any technique that enables machines to mimic intelligent behaviour.

Approach: May or may not learn from data — can use rules, logic, search algorithms.

Examples: Chess engines (minimax search), spell checkers, route planners, chatbots.
📊 Machine Learning
Definition: A subset of AI where systems learn from data rather than following hand-coded rules.

Approach: Given examples → find patterns → make predictions. Requires feature engineering.

Examples: Email spam filter, house price predictor, customer churn model.
⚡ Deep Learning
Definition: A subset of ML using multi-layer neural networks to learn hierarchical representations.

Approach: Raw data in → automatic feature extraction → prediction. Needs lots of data + GPU.

Examples: Image recognition, speech-to-text, large language models.
🔧
The Feature Engineering Divide
The biggest practical difference between classical ML and deep learning
Core Insight
Classical ML: you decide what to measure. Deep Learning: the model decides.
In classical machine learning, a data scientist must manually engineer features — e.g., for image classification, you might extract edge counts, colour histograms, or HOG descriptors. In deep learning, the network learns its own internal representations from raw pixels automatically.
Classical ML Pipeline
Raw Data → Feature Engineering → ML Model → Prediction

Pros: Interpretable, works with small data, fast to train
Cons: Requires domain expertise, features may miss patterns

sklearn.ensemble.RandomForestClassifier()
Deep Learning Pipeline
Raw Data → Neural Network (learns features) → Prediction

Pros: State-of-art on images/text/audio, no manual features
Cons: Needs huge data, expensive compute, black-box

torch.nn.Sequential(...)

Neural Networks & Deep Learning ⚡
How brains inspired machines — from a single artificial neuron to the deep architectures powering modern AI.
🧬 Neurons📐 Layers🔁 Backprop🖼️ CNNs🔊 RNNs
🧬
The Artificial Neuron
The building block of every neural network
From Biology to Math
A biological neuron fires when its inputs exceed a threshold. An artificial neuron does the same thing mathematically:

1. Receive inputs x₁, x₂, … xₙ (features or outputs from previous layer)
2. Multiply by weights w₁, w₂, … wₙ (learned parameters)
3. Add a bias b (shifts the activation threshold)
4. Apply activation function f — introduces non-linearity
5. Output y = f(w₁x₁ + w₂x₂ + … + wₙxₙ + b)
Activation:
Activation Functions
ReLU — max(0, x). Most common hidden layer activation. Fast and avoids vanishing gradients.

Sigmoid — 1/(1+e⁻ˣ). Squashes to (0,1). Used for binary output layers.

Tanh — (eˣ−e⁻ˣ)/(eˣ+e⁻ˣ). Squashes to (−1,1). Zero-centred, good for hidden layers.

Softmax — normalises to a probability distribution. Used for multi-class outputs.
Why Activation Functions?
Without a non-linear activation, stacking layers is pointless — the whole network collapses to a single linear transformation (Wₙ…W₂W₁x = W_combined·x).

Non-linearities let networks learn arbitrary complex functions. This is the Universal Approximation Theorem: a sufficiently wide single-hidden-layer network can approximate any continuous function.
📐
Network Architecture
Layers, depth, and width
📥 Input Layer
One neuron per feature. No computation — just passes raw data forward. For a 28×28 image, this is 784 neurons.
🔶 Hidden Layers
Where learning happens. Each layer learns increasingly abstract representations. "Deep" = many hidden layers.
📤 Output Layer
One neuron per class (classification) or one neuron (regression). Activation matches the task — softmax or sigmoid for classification.
🔁
How Networks Learn: Backpropagation
The algorithm behind every trained neural network
The Core Loop
Forward pass → compute loss → backward pass → update weights. Repeat millions of times.
Backpropagation uses the chain rule of calculus to compute how much each weight contributed to the error. Then gradient descent nudges each weight in the direction that reduces the error.
1️⃣ Forward Pass
Input flows through the network layer by layer. Each neuron computes its weighted sum + activation. The final output is the prediction ŷ.
2️⃣ Compute Loss
Compare ŷ to the true label y using a loss function (e.g. cross-entropy for classification, MSE for regression). Loss = how wrong we are.
3️⃣ Backward Pass
Compute ∂Loss/∂w for every weight using the chain rule — propagating error gradients backwards through the network.
4️⃣ Gradient Descent
Update each weight: w ← w − η·∂L/∂w where η is the learning rate. Repeat for each mini-batch. Eventually the network converges.
⚠️Vanishing gradients: in very deep networks, gradients can shrink to nearly zero before reaching early layers — those layers stop learning. Solutions: ReLU activations, batch normalisation, residual connections (ResNets).

Generative AI & LLMs ✨
The paradigm shift that put AI in everyone's hands — how large language models, diffusion models, and RLHF work under the hood.
🔤 LLMs🎨 Diffusion💬 RLHF🔮 Emergent🌐 Multimodal
✨
What is Generative AI?
From discriminative to generative models
📊 Discriminative Models
Learn the boundary between classes. Given input X, predict label Y.

Question: "Is this email spam?"
Models: Logistic regression, SVM, classifier neural nets.

These models classify — they don't create new data.
✨ Generative Models
Learn the underlying distribution of the data. Can generate new samples that look like training data.

Question: "Write me an email."
Models: GPT, DALL-E, Stable Diffusion, VAE, GAN.

These models create — text, images, audio, code, video.
The Paradigm Shift
Pre-2020: one model per task. Post-2020: one model, many tasks.
Traditional ML required a separate model for each task — a spam classifier, a sentiment analyser, a summariser. Foundation models like GPT-4 are trained once at massive scale and then prompted or fine-tuned to perform hundreds of different tasks. This is the essence of the generative AI revolution.
🔤
How Large Language Models Work
Tokens, embeddings, attention, and next-token prediction
The Core Task: Next Token Prediction
LLMs are trained on a deceptively simple task: predict the next token given all previous tokens. A token is roughly a word-piece (~¾ of a word on average). Trained on trillions of tokens, the model builds an extraordinarily rich internal model of language, knowledge, and reasoning.

At inference: you provide a prompt → the model samples from its predicted probability distribution over the vocabulary → appends that token → repeats until done. This is called autoregressive generation.
1️⃣ Tokenisation
Text is split into tokens using Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE). "unbelievable" might be ["un","believ","able"]. The vocabulary is typically 32k–100k tokens. Each token gets a unique integer ID.
2️⃣ Embeddings
Each token ID is mapped to a dense vector (e.g. 4096 dimensions). Similar words cluster together in this space. "king" − "man" + "woman" ≈ "queen" — meaning is encoded geometrically.
3️⃣ Transformer Layers
Self-attention lets each token attend to all previous tokens, building contextual representations. Stacked across 96+ layers (GPT-4), the model builds deep understanding of relationships.

🏋️
The Training Pipeline
How raw text becomes a helpful assistant
Stage 1 · Pre-training
Self-supervised learning on massive corpora
Train on hundreds of billions to trillions of tokens scraped from the web (Common Crawl), books, code, Wikipedia. Task: predict the next token. No human labels needed — the data is self-labelling. This is the most expensive step: GPT-3 cost ~$4.6M in compute. Result: a base model that knows a lot but doesn't follow instructions.
Stage 2 · Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)
Teaching the model to follow instructions
Human trainers write high-quality prompt → response examples. The base model is fine-tuned on these demonstrations. Result: a model that answers questions helpfully. But it can still be sycophantic, verbose, or unsafe without further alignment.
Stage 3 · RLHF
Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback
Human raters compare pairs of model outputs and rank them. A reward model is trained to predict human preferences. The LLM is then optimised using Proximal Policy Optimisation (PPO) to maximise the reward model's score. This is what transformed GPT-3 → InstructGPT → ChatGPT.
📌DPO (Direct Preference Optimisation) is a newer, simpler alternative to RLHF that skips the reward model entirely — directly optimising preferences. Used by Llama 3, Mistral, and others.
🎨
Diffusion Models for Image Generation
How Stable Diffusion and DALL-E create images from text
The Forward and Reverse Process
Forward process (training): take a real image → gradually add Gaussian noise over T steps until it's pure noise. The model never sees this process at inference — it's used to generate training pairs.

Reverse process (inference): start from pure noise → train a neural network (U-Net) to predict and remove noise step by step → after ~50 steps, a coherent image emerges.

Text conditioning: the text prompt is encoded (by a model like CLIP) and fed into the U-Net at each denoising step, guiding the image toward the description.
Key Models
DALL-E 2/3 (OpenAI) — CLIP-guided, integrated with ChatGPT
Stable Diffusion — open-source, runs locally, hugely popular
Midjourney — commercial, known for artistic quality
Imagen / Flux — Google / Black Forest Labs variants
Why Better Than GANs?
✅ More stable training (no mode collapse)
✅ Higher diversity and quality
✅ Better text alignment
✅ Easily conditioned on text, depth, pose, etc.
❌ Slower at inference (many denoising steps)
→ Latent Diffusion (Stable Diffusion) operates in compressed latent space for speed
🔮
Emergent Abilities & Scale
Capabilities that appear suddenly as models get larger
What Are Emergent Abilities?
As LLMs scale in parameters, training compute, and data, new capabilities appear without being explicitly trained for them. A small model cannot do multi-step arithmetic; a large one can. A small model fails at chain-of-thought reasoning; a large one succeeds with just a prompt hint.

Examples of emergent abilities: few-shot learning, chain-of-thought reasoning, code generation, analogy completion, multilingual transfer, theory of mind tasks.
⚠️Emergence is also controversial. Some researchers argue apparent emergence is an artifact of evaluation metrics — the capability was always improving smoothly, but hard metrics make it look sudden. The debate continues.

AI Ethics & Responsible AI ⚖️
The most important lesson in this module. The technical and non-technical challenges of ensuring AI systems are fair, safe, transparent, and accountable.
⚖️ Fairness👁️ Transparency🛡️ Safety🔒 Privacy🌍 Society
🌍
Why AI Ethics Matters
Real harm from real systems — not hypothetical
Ethics is Not Optional
AI systems make consequential decisions: who gets a loan, who gets bail, which CVs are shortlisted, which medical images are flagged. When these systems are biased, opaque, or unaccountable, they can cause serious harm at scale.

Unlike a human decision, an algorithmic decision is applied automatically to millions of people simultaneously. A biased algorithm doesn't discriminate one person at a time — it discriminates systematically.
📋 COMPAS (2016)
A recidivism prediction tool used by US courts was found to be twice as likely to falsely flag Black defendants as high-risk compared to white defendants. Real people received longer sentences.
👩‍💼 Amazon Hiring (2018)
Amazon scrapped an AI hiring tool after discovering it penalised CVs containing the word "women's" — trained on historical hiring data that reflected existing gender bias in tech.
👤 Facial Recognition
MIT study (2018) found commercial face recognition had error rates of 34% for dark-skinned women vs 0.8% for light-skinned men. Several innocent people have been wrongfully arrested.

Types of Bias in AI
Bias enters at every stage of the pipeline
Data Collection
Historical bias & sampling bias
Training data reflects past human decisions, which were often discriminatory. If historical hiring data shows men dominating tech roles, a model trained on it will perpetuate that pattern. Sampling bias occurs when training data doesn't represent the full population (e.g. mostly Western faces in a face recognition dataset).
Feature Selection
Proxy variables
A "neutral" feature like postcode can act as a proxy for race or income. The model learns to discriminate using the proxy even if race is explicitly excluded. Simply removing a sensitive attribute rarely removes the bias if correlated features remain.
Model Training
Optimisation for the wrong objective
Overall accuracy can be high while performance on minority subgroups is poor. A model 95% accurate overall could be 60% accurate for a minority group — the aggregate metric hides the disparity.
Deployment
Distribution shift & feedback loops
A model deployed into the world changes the world. Predictive policing sends more officers to predicted-crime areas → more arrests there → more training data from that area → reinforces the prediction. This feedback loop entrenches bias over time.
📐
Fairness Definitions — and Why They Conflict
You cannot satisfy all definitions simultaneously
Key Insight
Fairness is not one thing — mathematically incompatible definitions exist.
The "impossibility theorem" (Chouldechova, 2017) proves that when base rates differ between groups, you cannot simultaneously achieve demographic parity, equalised odds, AND calibration. Policy makers must choose which notion of fairness to prioritise — this is a value judgement, not a technical one.
DefinitionMeaningExample
Demographic ParityEqual positive prediction rates across groupsSame % of loan approvals for men and women
Equal OpportunityEqual true positive rates across groupsQualified people in both groups approved at same rate
Equalised OddsEqual TPR and FPR across groupsBoth false approvals and false rejections are equal
CalibrationPredicted probabilities are accurate within groupsA score of 0.7 means 70% chance for all groups
Individual FairnessSimilar people treated similarlyTwo candidates with equal qualifications get equal scores

Explainability & Transparency
The black-box problem — and solutions
Why Explainability Matters
🏦 Regulation: EU GDPR's "right to explanation" — individuals can request reasons for automated decisions

⚕️ Healthcare: Doctors need to understand why a model flagged a diagnosis before acting

⚖️ Law: Courts require explainable reasoning for decisions affecting liberty

🔒 Trust: Users won't adopt a system they can't understand or audit
Explainability Techniques
LIME — Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations: fits a simple interpretable model locally around a prediction

SHAP — SHapley Additive exPlanations: assigns each feature a contribution score based on game theory

Attention maps — visualise which input tokens/pixels the model attended to

Counterfactuals — "what would need to change for a different outcome?"

AI Governance & Regulation
How governments and organisations are responding
🇪🇺 EU AI Act (2024)
World's first comprehensive AI regulation. Classifies AI by risk level: unacceptable → high → limited → minimal. Bans real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces. Requires transparency for high-risk AI in hiring, credit, healthcare.
🇬🇧 UK AI Safety Institute
Established 2023 to evaluate frontier AI systems before deployment. Focus on catastrophic and societal risks. Less prescriptive than EU — principle-based rather than rule-based. Hosted the first AI Safety Summit at Bletchley Park.
🏢 Corporate Frameworks
Google's AI Principles, Microsoft's Responsible AI Standard, Anthropic's Constitutional AI. Internal red-teaming, bias audits, staged rollouts. Still largely voluntary — critics argue self-regulation is insufficient.
✅As an AI practitioner, you are responsible for the systems you build. Technical skill without ethical awareness is dangerous at scale. Responsible AI is everyone's job — not just a compliance team.
