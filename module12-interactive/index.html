🗺️ Intro to XAI & Interpretability

Why black-box models aren't enough — and the landscape of tools to open them up.

Why Does Explainability Matter?

A model with 99% accuracy is useless if you can't trust, audit, debug, or legally justify its decisions. XAI addresses four core needs:

⚖️
Legal & Compliance
GDPR Article 22 gives people the right to an explanation for automated decisions. Finance, healthcare, and hiring are regulated sectors where "the model said so" is not acceptable.
🐛
Debugging
If your model is using the wrong features (e.g. predicting pneumonia risk from "no asthma" — because asthmatics are fast-tracked), explainability reveals the spurious correlation.
🤝
Trust & Adoption
Doctors, judges, and loan officers won't trust and use a system they can't understand. Explanations build the human trust needed for real-world deployment.
🔬
Scientific Discovery
In drug discovery and genomics, we want to learn from the model — which molecular features predict binding? Explainability turns the model into a hypothesis generator.
⚡
Bias Detection
Models trained on historical data inherit societal biases. XAI reveals if gender or race (or proxies) drive predictions in hiring, lending, or criminal justice models.
🎯
Model Improvement
Understanding what drives predictions reveals data quality issues, feature engineering opportunities, and failure modes you'd never find by looking at accuracy metrics alone.

Interpretability vs Explainability

Interpretability

The degree to which a human can understand the model's mechanism directly. An intrinsic property of the model itself.

e.g. A linear regression model: you can read off coefficients and understand exactly why each prediction was made.

Explainability

A post-hoc approximation or summary of a model's behaviour, generated after training using a separate explanation method.

e.g. SHAP values applied to a trained XGBoost model: you get an approximation of what drove each prediction.

There is often an accuracy-interpretability trade-off: simpler, interpretable models (linear, shallow trees) are easier to understand but may be less accurate than complex black-box models (deep nets, random forests).

🔬 Local Explanations

LIME, Anchors, and counterfactuals — explaining one prediction at a time.

What is a Local Explanation?

A local explanation answers: "Why did the model make this specific prediction for this specific input?" It does not describe the model overall — only behaviour in the neighbourhood of one data point.

Analogy: you don't need to understand how an aircraft engine works globally — you need to know why this alarm is going off right now. Local explanations are actionable and specific.

LIME — Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations

LIME fits a simple surrogate model (e.g. linear regression) in the local neighbourhood of the prediction being explained. The surrogate's coefficients become the explanation.

① Pick point
② Perturb
③ Black-box predict
④ Weight by distance
⑤ Fit surrogate
⑥ Read coefficients
Select the instance x you want to explain — e.g. a loan application that was denied.
Generate many perturbed samples x' around x by randomly turning features on/off (for text/images) or adding noise (for tabular data). ~1,000–5,000 samples.
Run all perturbed samples x' through the black-box model f(x') to get predictions. This treats the model as a function we can query but not inspect.
Weight each perturbed sample by its distance from x: nearby samples matter more. Typically use exponential kernel: weight = exp(−d(x,x')²/σ²).
Fit a weighted linear regression on {x', f(x'), weights}. The linear model is now a local approximation of the black-box around x.
The linear regression coefficients βᵢ are the LIME explanation: positive β means feature i pushed the prediction up; negative means it pushed it down. These are shown as a bar chart to the user.

Anchors — Rule-Based Local Explanations

Anchors finds an if-then rule (an "anchor") that is sufficient to guarantee the prediction, regardless of other feature values.

IF income > 45k AND no_late_payments = True THEN prediction = "Approved" (precision ≥ 95%)

How Anchors Works

  • Start with the most important feature
  • Iteratively add features to the rule
  • Stop when the rule has sufficient precision (fraction of times it's correct)
  • Measures coverage: how often the anchor applies to the input space

Anchors vs LIME

  • Anchors gives a rule (binary, discrete) — easy for non-experts to understand
  • LIME gives coefficients — shows direction & magnitude of each feature
  • Anchors is sufficient: rule guarantees prediction holds even if other features change
  • LIME is a linear approximation: only valid locally
🎯
Precision
How often does the anchor correctly predict the class? A precision of 0.95 means the rule holds 95% of the time when its conditions are met.
🗺️
Coverage
What fraction of the input space does the anchor apply to? High coverage = more generally useful rule. Anchors trades off precision vs coverage.
🏝️
Beam Search
Anchors uses beam search (with bandit algorithms for efficiency) to find rules that maximise coverage subject to a precision threshold.

Counterfactual Explanations — "What Would Need to Change?"

A counterfactual explanation answers: "What is the smallest change to the input that would flip the prediction?"

Original: income=35k, debt=80%, late_payments=2 → DENIED
Counterfactual: income=35k, debt=55%, late_payments=0 → APPROVED

Desirable Properties

  • Proximity — small change from original input
  • Sparsity — change as few features as possible
  • Actionability — changes must be feasible (can't change age)
  • Plausibility — result should be a realistic data point

Methods

  • DICE (Diverse Counterfactual Explanations) — generates multiple diverse counterfactuals
  • Wachter et al. — minimise loss + distance objective
  • FACE — finds feasible, actionable counterfactuals along data manifold
  • Growing Spheres — searches expanding neighbourhood for flipped predictions

🌍 Global Explanations

SHAP, Permutation Feature Importance, and Partial Dependence Plots — understanding the model as a whole.

What is a Global Explanation?

A global explanation answers: "How does the model behave across all inputs?" — which features does it rely on, and how does changing a feature's value affect the output on average?

Use cases

  • Model auditing — what is driving predictions overall?
  • Feature selection — which inputs matter most?
  • Bias detection — is gender or race influencing predictions?
  • Regulatory reporting — explain the model to stakeholders

Key methods

  • SHAP — game-theory based attribution, consistent
  • Permutation Feature Importance (PFI) — shuffle-based
  • Partial Dependence Plots (PDP) — marginalised effect
  • ICE plots — individual-level PDP curves

SHAP — SHapley Additive exPlanations

SHAP is grounded in cooperative game theory. Each feature is a "player" and SHAP computes their fair contribution to the prediction.

φᵢ = Σ [|S|!(n−|S|−1)/n!] · [f(S∪{i}) − f(S)] over all subsets S not containing i

Key SHAP properties: Efficiency (attributions sum to prediction − baseline), Symmetry (equal features get equal attribution), Dummy (features with no effect get zero), Additivity (can decompose model). These axioms make SHAP the theoretically principled choice.

SHAP Summary Plot (Beeswarm)

Each dot is one data point. Horizontal position = SHAP value (impact on prediction). Colour = feature value (red=high, blue=low).

Permutation Feature Importance (PFI)

Shuffle one feature at a time. If accuracy drops a lot — the feature was important. If accuracy barely changes — the feature is unimportant (or redundant).

① Baseline score
② Shuffle feature j
③ Score again
④ Importance = drop
⑤ Repeat all features
Compute the baseline model score (e.g. AUC=0.87) on the held-out test set without any shuffling.
Randomly shuffle feature j's values across all test samples — breaking any real relationship between that feature and the target.
Re-score the model on the shuffled data. If AUC drops to 0.71, the feature contributed 0.87−0.71=0.16 to performance.
Importance(j) = baseline_score − shuffled_score. Repeat shuffling multiple times and average to reduce variance.
Repeat for every feature. Plot a bar chart ranked by importance. Negative importance = shuffling this feature accidentally improves performance (suggests multicollinearity).

⚠️ PFI can be misleading with correlated features: shuffling one correlated feature still leaves the other as a proxy. Use conditional permutation importance to handle correlations properly.

Partial Dependence Plots (PDP)

A PDP shows the marginalised effect of one feature on the prediction, averaged over all other features. Shows direction and shape of effect across the feature's full range.

PDP(xₛ) = E_{x_c}[f(xₛ, x_c)] ≈ (1/n) Σᵢ f(xₛ, xᵢ_c)

Age effect: risk decreases sharply until ~35, then increases slightly with age. The U-shaped curve suggests middle-aged applicants are lowest risk in this model.

ICE Plots — Individual Conditional Expectation

ICE plots show the PDP curve for each individual data point — revealing heterogeneity that the average (PDP) masks.

PDP (average line)

Shows average effect. Hides subgroup differences. Can be flat even when individual effects vary wildly.

ICE (individual lines)

Shows every individual's curve. Can reveal: some subgroups increase while others decrease (interaction effects). PDP = mean of ICE curves.

SHAP vs PFI — When to Use Which

✅
SHAP Strengths
Consistent, locally accurate, handles correlated features better. Can be aggregated globally OR used locally per instance. Theoretically grounded via Shapley axioms.
⚠️
SHAP Weaknesses
Slow for large datasets (TreeSHAP is fast for tree models). Can be unstable with highly correlated features. Not universally well-calibrated for all models.
✅
PFI Strengths
Simple to compute, model-agnostic, measures actual impact on performance (not just prediction). Easy to explain to non-technical stakeholders.
⚠️
PFI Weaknesses
Misleading with correlated features (shuffling one leaves others as proxies). Requires labelled test data. Doesn't tell you direction or shape of the effect.

🧩 Model-Specific Methods

Decision tree paths, linear coefficients, attention weights, and intrinsically interpretable architectures.

Model-Specific vs Model-Agnostic

Model-Specific

Exploit the internal structure of a particular model type. Exact, fast, but not transferable to other model families.

  • Decision tree paths
  • Linear model coefficients
  • Attention weights (Transformers)
  • Gradient-based saliency (neural nets)

Model-Agnostic

Query the model as a black box — work for any model, but are approximations.

  • LIME, Anchors, Counterfactuals
  • SHAP (KernelSHAP is model-agnostic; TreeSHAP is model-specific)
  • PFI, PDPs, ICE plots

Decision Trees — Fully Transparent

Decision trees are intrinsically interpretable. Every prediction is explained by the path from root to leaf. Click a leaf node to see the full decision path.

Click a leaf node to trace the decision path for that prediction.

⚠️ Deep trees become uninterpretable. The "right to explanation" in practice favours trees with depth ≤ 5. Random forests sacrifice this direct interpretability — use SHAP on top.

Linear Model Coefficients

In a linear model, each coefficient βᵢ directly tells you: "holding all else constant, a one-unit increase in xᵢ changes the output by βᵢ."

ŷ = β₀ + β₁·income + β₂·debt + β₃·credit_age + … + βₙ·feature_n

⚠️ Coefficients are only meaningful when features are on the same scale (standardised). Raw coefficients for income ($) and age (years) are incomparable. Always standardise first or use standardised coefficients.

📏
Standardisation
Subtract mean, divide by std. Then |β| reflects relative importance regardless of unit. Required before comparing coefficients across features.
🎲
Multicollinearity
Correlated features cause coefficient instability — both get inflated/deflated arbitrary values. Use VIF to detect it. L2 regularisation helps stabilise coefficients.
🔗
Logistic Regression
Coefficients are log-odds. exp(β) = odds ratio. A coefficient of 0.3 means odds of positive class multiply by exp(0.3)=1.35 per unit increase in that feature.

Attention Weights & Neural Interpretability

Attention mechanisms in Transformers produce weights over input tokens — often visualised as a heatmap. But attention ≠ explanation: high attention ≠ causally important.

Sentiment

Gradient-Based Methods

  • Vanilla Gradient Saliency — ∂ output/∂ input pixel. Shows which pixels affect output most.
  • Integrated Gradients — average gradients from baseline to input; satisfies completeness axiom.
  • GradCAM — uses gradients w.r.t. conv feature maps to produce class activation maps.

Probing & Concept Methods

  • Probing classifiers — train a linear model on internal representations to test what the network "knows".
  • TCAV — test with concept activation vectors; does the network use a human-defined concept?
  • Mechanistic interpretability — reverse-engineer specific circuits inside the network.

Choosing Interpretable vs Black-Box

🏦
Credit Scoring
Prefer scorecard (linear model) — regulators require adverse action notices with specific reasons. Logistic regression with manual feature engineering often matches XGBoost accuracy here.
🏥
Clinical Decision Support
Clinicians need to trust and override the model. Shallow decision tree or risk score (logistic) with SHAP for complex cases. RETAIN model for EHR sequential data.
🛡️
Fraud Detection
Performance is paramount — use XGBoost or neural net. Apply SHAP locally to explain flagged transactions to analysts. Global PFI for model auditing.
🔬
Drug Discovery
GNN + SHAP or Integrated Gradients to highlight which molecular substructures drive predictions. Interpretability here serves scientific discovery, not legal compliance.

⚖️ Fairness, Bias & XAI in Production

Fairness metrics, bias detection, algorithmic accountability, and deploying XAI responsibly.

Where Does Bias Come From?

Bias in ML systems is not just a technical problem — it reflects and amplifies social inequities present in data and processes. Click each source to understand it.

📊
Historical Bias
Training data reflects past discriminatory decisions. A model trained on historical hiring data will perpetuate past gender or racial bias — it learns "who was hired" not "who should be hired".
📉
Representation Bias
Minority groups are under-represented in training data. Model performs poorly on these groups — e.g. facial recognition with 99% accuracy on white males but 65% on dark-skinned women.
🔗
Proxy Features
Even if gender/race are excluded, correlated features act as proxies: postcode correlates with race, name correlates with gender. Removing protected attributes is not enough.
📋
Measurement Bias
The label or feature measurement process is biased. Arrest rates (not crime rates) are used as a proxy for criminality — police patrol some areas more, creating a feedback loop.
🔄
Feedback Loops
Model output influences future data collection. Predictive policing sends police to predicted crime areas → more arrests there → model sees more crime there → feedback amplifies.
🎯
Evaluation Bias
Benchmarks don't represent deployment population. A model evaluated on benchmark X may be biased against groups under-represented in X but common in the real use case.

Fairness Metrics — Three Definitions

Different fairness criteria often conflict. Understanding each is essential before choosing one. Click each metric to see the formula and tradeoffs.

P(ŷ=1|A=0) = P(ŷ=1|A=1)

Demographic Parity: the model approves equal proportions across groups A=0 (e.g. female) and A=1 (e.g. male). Does not account for base rate differences. Can force inaccuracy.

The Impossibility Theorem

Chouldechova (2017) and Kleinberg et al. (2017) proved that most common fairness criteria are mutually incompatible when base rates differ across groups.

⚠️ You cannot simultaneously achieve Demographic Parity + Equal Opportunity + Calibration when positive rates differ between groups. Choosing a fairness criterion is a value judgement, not a technical decision.

Example: COMPAS Recidivism Tool

ProPublica showed COMPAS had higher false positive rates for Black defendants. Northpointe replied it was calibrated (equal predictive accuracy per score). Both were correct — they used different fairness definitions.

What to do?

  • Involve affected communities in metric choice
  • Audit across all fairness criteria; report tradeoffs
  • Consider whether the task should be automated at all
  • Document the chosen fairness definition and why

Regulatory Landscape

🇪🇺 EU AI Act (2024)

High-risk AI systems (credit, hiring, criminal justice, biometrics) must have human oversight, technical documentation, and provide meaningful explanations to affected persons.

🇪🇺 GDPR Art. 22

Right not to be subject to solely automated decisions with significant effects. Right to meaningful information about the logic involved (not a full technical explanation).

🇺🇸 Fair Credit Reporting Act

Adverse action notices must state specific reasons for credit denial. "The algorithm said so" is not compliant — must name actual factors like "too many inquiries".
