🗺️ Introduction to Explainability & Interpretability

What it is, why it matters, who needs it, and the landscape of approaches.

You've Trained a Great Model… but is the Job Done?

Imagine a data scientist who has:

  • Cleaned and preprocessed messy data
  • Engineered fancy new features
  • Identified the best model by tuning hyperparameters
  • Got great performance on the test set

Does great predictive performance alone qualify as a "job well done"? In many applications — no. When AI impacts real people's lives, we also need to understand why the model makes its decisions.

In high-stakes domains — medicine, finance, law — being able to explain and understand how a prediction is made can be just as important as accuracy.

The Complexity Problem — Black Box Models

Modern ML models achieve extraordinary accuracy, but complexity makes them hard to understand and trust.

📈
Easy to Interpret

Linear / Logistic Regression — weights directly show feature contributions.
e.g. y = 3.5x₁ + 4.2x₂

🌲
Harder to Interpret

Ensemble models (Random Forests, GBMs) — complex structure, need feature importance to understand role of each feature.

🧠
Really Hard (Black Box)

Deep Neural Networks — millions/billions of parameters, no straightforward input→output explanation.

Complexity gives extraordinary accuracy — but complexity makes it difficult to understand and hence trust the models.

Interpretability vs Explainability

🔍 Interpretable Model

A model that can be directly explained and interpreted by human experts. Uses "white box" models. Trade-off: limited complexity reduces performance.

Examples: Linear Regression, Logistic Regression, single Decision Tree

💡 Explainable Model

A model that is too complex for a human to understand directly. Additional tools/techniques are needed to "explain" how the model makes predictions.

Examples: Random Forest + SHAP, Neural Network + LIME

There is an inherent accuracy–interpretability trade-off: simpler, interpretable models are easier to understand but often less accurate than complex black-box models.

Algorithms increasingly make or inform decisions that directly affect people. This creates demand for transparency:

🏦

Controls individuals' ability to get a mortgage, car loan, or business funding. A rejected applicant deserves to know why.

🏥

⚖️

Risk-assessment tools determine bail, sentencing, and parole. Opaque decisions here have profound human rights implications.

Bias Example: A model trained on historical employee data may learn that men are promoted more than women — and then reinforce that bias in future promotion decisions. Explainability is how we detect this.

Drivers of Model Explainability

The need for explainability is driven by multiple factors. Click each driver to expand:

⚖️ Regulatory Compliance▼

Governments and recognised bodies impose regulations requiring transparency in automated decision-making.

  • EU AI Act (2024) — High-risk AI systems must be transparent and auditable
  • Fair Credit Reporting Act — US lenders must provide reasons for credit denials

High-stakes applications demand transparency. Explanations help humans identify where predictions are coming from — and where the model is going wrong.

Example: A hospital using a diagnostic model needs to be able to scrutinise and override it if it behaves unexpectedly.

🤝 Ethics & Trust▼

Explainability ties directly to ethical implications: equal opportunity, fairness, human rights.

  • Recommendation systems: explanations detect bias towards certain content
  • Classifiers: explanations reveal unfair behaviour towards protected groups
  • In Education, Healthcare and Defence: transparency builds trust between stakeholders and AI systems
🐛 Debugging Models▼

If a model is using the wrong features, you'll never find out by looking at accuracy alone. Explainability reveals spurious correlations.

Classic example: A pneumonia risk model learned that "no asthma" = lower risk — because asthmatics were fast-tracked to intensive care and survived. The model was wrong for the right-looking reasons.

🔬 Local Explanations

LIME and Anchors — explaining one prediction at a time, in a model-agnostic way.

What is a Local Explanation?

A local explanation explains a single output of a machine learning model — not how the model behaves generally, but why it made this specific prediction for this specific input.

🏥

Why did a medical AI recommend this diagnosis for this patient?

🏦

Why was this applicant denied a loan by their bank?

⚖️

Why was bail denied for this defendant?

Local explanations are often personal explanations. When individuals are personally affected by model outputs — in Law, Medicine, and Finance — those specific predictions are subject to greater scrutiny. GDPR Art. 22 gives EU citizens the right to a local explanation.

Types of Local Explanations

Feature Weighting

Each input feature receives a weight showing how much it contributed to the prediction. e.g. LIME coefficients.

Feature Subsets

Return only the most important features (without weights). Useful when the feature space is very large or not human-friendly.

Rule-Based

An if-then rule: "IF income > 45k AND late_payments = 0 THEN Approved." Best of both worlds — reduces features AND adds context. e.g. Anchors.

LIME — Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations

LIME is a feature weighting explanation method. The core intuition: even if a black-box model has a complex global decision boundary, it can often be approximated locally by a simple linear model.

"The decisions of a black box classifier with a complex global decision boundary can be explained locally by the weights of a linear approximation to the boundary in a neighbourhood around the point of interest."

The LIME Algorithm

① Pick point
② Perturb
③ Black-box predict
④ Weight
⑤ Fit surrogate
⑥ Explain
Step 1: Choose an interpretable data representation x′ of instance x (e.g. word presence/absence for text, super-pixels for images, continuous features for tabular). Choose a class of interpretable models G (e.g. linear models, decision trees). Choose a kernel π_x that defines what "local" means around x′.
Step 2: Sample around x′ by perturbing it — ~1,000–5,000 samples. For tabular data: add Gaussian noise. For text: zero words randomly. For images: zero out super-pixels. This gives us a dataset z′ of perturbed interpretable inputs.
Step 3: Get predictions f(z) for all perturbed samples using the black-box model. We treat f as a function we can query but cannot inspect. Each perturbed sample z now has a predicted label f(z).
Step 4: Weight each perturbed sample by its proximity to x using the kernel π_x. For an exponential kernel: π_x(z) = exp(−D(x,z)²/σ²). Nearby samples get high weights; distant ones get low weights.
Step 5: Fit the interpretable model g ∈ G on the weighted dataset {z′, f(z), π_x(z)}. Use locally-weighted squared loss: L(f, g, π_x) = Σ π_x(z)(f(z) − g(z′))². This gives a linear model that approximates f near x.
Step 6: The linear regression coefficients w_g are the explanation — positive w means the feature pushed the prediction up; negative means it pushed it down. Only the K largest coefficients are reported (for readability).
0.30
LIME coefficients at x=0.30: feature₁=+0.72, feature₂=−0.41

LIME — The Maths

For a continuous feature space (e.g. tabular data), the LIME objective is:

ξ(x) = argming∈G L(f, g, π_x) + Ω(g)

where Ω(g) is a complexity penalty (e.g. number of features in the linear model). Using an exponential kernel and locally-weighted squared loss:

π_x(z) = exp(−D(x,z)² / σ²)
L(f, g, π_x) = Σz,z′∈Z π_x(z) (f(z) − g(z′))²

The local surrogate model is linear: g(z′) = w_gT · z′ — and we train it on the perturbed samples weighted by their proximity to x.

LIME — Advantages & Disadvantages

✅ Advantages
  • Same explanation method for any underlying model — consistency for lay audiences
  • Simple, intuitive explanations (bar charts)
  • Works on tabular, image, and text data
  • Well-maintained Python library (lime)
  • Model-agnostic: no need to understand the black-box internals
❌ Disadvantages
  • High variability: two adjacent points can produce quite different explanations — results are not stable
  • Kernel choice substantially impacts the explanation and is hard to justify precisely
  • Relies on the assumption that the decision boundary is locally linear — this may not hold
  • The "neighbourhood" definition is ambiguous for high-dimensional data

Anchors — Rule-Based Local Explanations

Anchors (by the same authors as LIME) is a rule-based explanation method. Instead of fitting a surrogate model, it finds an if-then rule that is sufficient to lock in a prediction.

IF income > 45k AND no_late_payments = True THEN prediction = "Approved" (precision ≥ 95%)

The Parts of an Anchor

Predicates

A feature predicate is a rule about a feature: e.g. A₁: x₁ ≥ 3.4 and A₂: x₂ ∈ [0,3]. The anchor A = {A₁, A₂} — if all rules are satisfied, A(x)=1.

Coverage

The region of input space where all predicates are true. A larger coverage means the anchor applies to more inputs — making it a more general explanation.

Precision

Prec(A) = proportion of perturbed samples z where A(z)=1 and f(z)=f(x). A precision of 0.95 means the rule guarantees the prediction 95% of the time.

Choosing the Anchor

Of all anchors with sufficient precision (Prec(A) ≥ τ), choose the one with the largest coverage. The set of predicates with the largest coverage around the point are the rules which best explain its classification. Anchors uses beam search + reinforcement learning to efficiently find this anchor.

① Point of interest
② Perturb
③ Build candidates
④ Beam search
⑤ Return anchor
Start with a point of interest x and a desired precision threshold τ (e.g. 0.95).
Sample perturbations z around x using distribution P(z). Each z is a neighbour of x (with some features changed).
Generate candidate anchors — all possible combinations of feature predicates. Check each against the perturbed samples using the black-box model f(z).
Use beam search to efficiently explore the space of possible anchors — keeping only the best candidates at each step. Bandits are used to estimate precision efficiently without exhaustive sampling.
Return the anchor A with precision ≥ τ and maximum coverage. This is the rule that best explains the prediction — it applies to the widest area while still being reliable.

Anchors — Advantages & Disadvantages

✅ Advantages
  • Easy to understand — if-then rules are intuitive for non-experts
  • Coverage allows you to compare how general different explanations are
  • Model-agnostic — works with any black-box
  • Well-implemented in the anchor-exp library
❌ Disadvantages
  • Currently primarily implemented for tabular data only
  • Coverage may not be meaningful in high-dimensional/image domains
  • Runtime depends on black-box efficiency — every perturbation requires a call to f(z)
  • Highly complex decision boundaries may result in anchors that cover very little of the input space

LIME vs Anchors in summary: LIME gives you how much each feature contributed (weights/magnitudes). Anchors gives you which conditions lock in a prediction (rules/coverage). Use LIME when you want to understand feature contributions; use Anchors when you want actionable, interpretable rules.

🌍 Global Explanations

Permutation Feature Importance and SHAP — understanding the whole model, not just one prediction.

What is a Global Explanation?

A global explanation explains the overall behaviour of a model — not why it made a specific prediction, but which features it generally relies on and how.

Feature Attribution

"What features of the data is the model generally relying on?" — e.g. PFI, SHAP global

Model Distillation

"What is a simplified version of our model that a human can understand?" — e.g. distilling a neural network into a decision tree

In this lesson we focus on feature attribution — post-hoc, model-agnostic methods that peer into the black-box model and tease out which features it's using.

Motivating Example: Wisconsin Breast Cancer Dataset

The Wisconsin Diagnosis Breast Cancer Dataset (WDBCD) contains 569 samples of breast tissue, each labelled malignant or benign. There are 30 features computed from a digitised image of each sample's cell nuclei.

Feature Description
Mean radiusMean of distances from centre to points on the perimeter
Mean perimeterMean average of the nuclei perimeters
Mean areaMean area of the nuclei
Mean smoothnessMean local variation in radius lengths
Worst perimeterLargest (worst) perimeter value across cell nuclei

A Random Forest trained on this data achieves ~97% test accuracy. It consists of 100 trees — far too complex for any human to interpret directly. This is why we need global explanation methods.

Use case: An oncologist wants to use the classifier as a diagnostic tool. Before trusting it, she needs to know: "Is this model using sensible features to make its decisions?" A global explanation answers that.

Permutation Feature Importance (PFI)

PFI answers: "What features is the model generally relying on?" The intuition: if we randomly shuffle a feature column, a model that depends on that feature will perform much worse.

① Choose feature
② Shuffle column
③ Measure drop
④ Importance score
⑤ Repeat all features
Choose a feature to compute importance for — e.g. "mean smoothness".
Randomly shuffle the values in that column only, leaving all other columns intact. This breaks the relationship between this feature and the target, while keeping the model unchanged.
Measure model performance before and after shuffling (e.g. accuracy or F1). A large drop means the feature was important.
The importance score = drop in performance = (original accuracy) − (accuracy after shuffle). Repeat N times and average to reduce randomness from shuffling.
Repeat for every feature. Plot the results as a bar chart (or box plot for multiple repetitions) to show relative feature importance across the whole model.

Click "Run PFI" to see permutation feature importance results on the breast cancer dataset. Features with higher importance scores caused a larger accuracy drop when shuffled.

PFI — Advantages & Words of Warning

✅ Advantages
  • Fast to calculate
  • Works with classification and regression
  • Gives very compressed representation of the model
  • Post-hoc, model-agnostic method
  • Available in sklearn.inspection and eli5
⚠️ Words of Warning
  • Randomness: shuffling varies each run — repeat and average to stabilise
  • Train vs test: unclear which to use (see Christoph Molnar's "Interpretable ML" book)
  • Correlated features: shuffling a feature correlated with another may produce unrealistic samples (e.g. height=2m, weight=40kg) — and importance gets split between them

SHAP — The Game Theory Foundation

SHAP uses Shapley Values from cooperative game theory. The analogy: a team of superheroes wins a prize. How do we fairly distribute the prize based on each hero's contribution?

Suppose we have 4 superheroes: Mastermind (M), Bulletproof (B), Eagle Eyes (EE), and Lightning Bolt (LB). Any subset (coalition) of them can play a game and win a prize y(S).

  • Full team y(M,B,EE,LB) = 17 | Mastermind alone y(M) = 2
  • The Shapley Value of Mastermind = his weighted average marginal contribution across all possible coalitions he could join
  • This is a provably fair way to attribute contributions
φᵢ = ΣS ⊆ N\{i} |S|!(|N|−|S|−1)! / |N|! × (y(S∪{i}) − y(S))

In ML: the "players" are features; the "prize" is the model output (prediction). SHAP computes each feature's fair contribution to the prediction.

SHAP — Computing Marginal Contributions

Consider a model predicting house prices with 3 features: bedrooms (x₁), floor area (x₂), garden (x₃). Average price in training data = £250,000.

A new house has: x₁=6 bedrooms, x₂=200m², x₃=no garden. Model predicts £320,000.

To compute the Shapley Value of bedrooms (x₁=6), we look at how adding x₁ to different coalitions changes the prediction. We handle "missing" features by substituting their average value:

f(x₁=6, x₂=E[x₂], x₃=E[x₃]) − f(x₁=E[x₁], x₂=E[x₂], x₃=E[x₃])
f(x₁=6, x₂=200, x₃=E[x₃]) − f(x₁=E[x₁], x₂=200, x₃=E[x₃])
f(x₁=6, x₂=E[x₂], x₃=0) − f(x₁=E[x₁], x₂=E[x₂], x₃=0)
f(x₁=6, x₂=200, x₃=0) − f(x₁=E[x₁], x₂=200, x₃=0)

Plug these marginal contributions into the Shapley formula (weighted average) → SHAP value for x₁ for this specific house.

Because we use the actual value x₁=6 and information about this specific house (x₂=200m², etc.), the SHAP value is local to this house. Averaging the magnitude of SHAP values across all houses gives the global explanation.

SHAP — Algorithm & Implementation

Formal algorithm: for each sample i and feature j, compute the weighted average marginal contribution:

φᵢⱼ = ΣS ⊆ P\{xᵢⱼ} |S|!(p−|S|−1)! / p! × (f(S∪{xᵢⱼ}) − f(S))

where P is the full set of features, S is the coalition (features included), and "missing" features use their expected value E[xⱼ].

Implementation — Three Explainer Types

TreeExplainer

For tree-based models: Random Forest, XGBoost, LightGBM. Fast exact computation.

DeepExplainer

For deep learning models (TensorFlow, PyTorch). Uses backpropagation-based approximation.

KernelExplainer

Truly model-agnostic. Works with any model but is slower — uses LIME-style sampling to approximate Shapley values.

SHAP — Local & Global Explanations

SHAP computes both local (per-sample) and global explanations. The beeswarm plot shows local SHAP values for all samples — each dot is one sample, colour shows feature value (red=high, blue=low):

SHAP Global Bar Chart (mean |SHAP|)

The global explanation = mean of the magnitude of SHAP values across all samples. Using magnitude (not mean) captures features that have large effects in either direction.

⚠️ SHAP Word of Warning: While more sophisticated than PFI, SHAP is not perfect. Research shows it is possible to create intentionally misleading SHAP explanations that hide biases — see "Fooling LIME and SHAP: Adversarial Attacks on Post hoc Explanation Methods" (Slack et al., 2020). If you are the data scientist, this is not a problem — but if you are receiving an explanation, verify the methodology.

PFI vs SHAP — When to Use Which?

Use PFI when…
  • You need a quick, approximate global importance
  • Features are relatively uncorrelated
  • You want to use sklearn natively
  • Speed matters more than precision
Use SHAP when…
  • You need both local and global explanations
  • You want to see feature interactions and direction of effects
  • Correlated features are present (SHAP handles these better)
  • You need theoretically grounded, fair attribution

🧩 Model-Specific Methods

Decision tree paths, linear coefficients, attention weights, and intrinsically interpretable architectures.

Model-Specific vs Model-Agnostic

Model-Specific

Exploit the internal structure of a particular model type. Exact, fast, but not transferable to other model families.

  • Decision tree paths
  • Linear model coefficients
  • Attention weights (Transformers)
  • Gradient-based saliency (neural nets)

Model-Agnostic

Query the model as a black box — work for any model, but are approximations.

  • LIME, Anchors, Counterfactuals
  • SHAP (KernelSHAP is model-agnostic; TreeSHAP is model-specific)
  • PFI, PDPs, ICE plots

Decision Trees — Fully Transparent

Decision trees are intrinsically interpretable. Every prediction is explained by the path from root to leaf. Click a leaf node to see the full decision path.

Click a leaf node to trace the decision path for that prediction.

⚠️ Deep trees become uninterpretable. The "right to explanation" in practice favours trees with depth ≤ 5. Random forests sacrifice this direct interpretability — use SHAP on top.

Linear Model Coefficients

In a linear model, each coefficient βᵢ directly tells you: "holding all else constant, a one-unit increase in xᵢ changes the output by βᵢ."

ŷ = β₀ + β₁·income + β₂·debt + β₃·credit_age + … + βₙ·feature_n

⚠️ Coefficients are only meaningful when features are on the same scale (standardised). Raw coefficients for income ($) and age (years) are incomparable. Always standardise first or use standardised coefficients.

📏
Standardisation
Subtract mean, divide by std. Then |β| reflects relative importance regardless of unit. Required before comparing coefficients across features.
🎲
Multicollinearity
Correlated features cause coefficient instability — both get inflated/deflated arbitrary values. Use VIF to detect it. L2 regularisation helps stabilise coefficients.
🔗
Logistic Regression
Coefficients are log-odds. exp(β) = odds ratio. A coefficient of 0.3 means odds of positive class multiply by exp(0.3)=1.35 per unit increase in that feature.

Attention Weights & Neural Interpretability

Attention mechanisms in Transformers produce weights over input tokens — often visualised as a heatmap. But attention ≠ explanation: high attention ≠ causally important.

Sentiment

Gradient-Based Methods

  • Vanilla Gradient Saliency — ∂ output/∂ input pixel. Shows which pixels affect output most.
  • Integrated Gradients — average gradients from baseline to input; satisfies completeness axiom.
  • GradCAM — uses gradients w.r.t. conv feature maps to produce class activation maps.

Probing & Concept Methods

  • Probing classifiers — train a linear model on internal representations to test what the network "knows".
  • TCAV — test with concept activation vectors; does the network use a human-defined concept?
  • Mechanistic interpretability — reverse-engineer specific circuits inside the network.

⚖️ Fairness, Bias & XAI in Production

Fairness metrics, bias detection, algorithmic accountability, and deploying XAI responsibly.

Where Does Bias Come From?

Bias in ML systems is not just a technical problem — it reflects and amplifies social inequities present in data and processes. Click each source to understand it.

📊
Historical Bias
Training data reflects past discriminatory decisions. A model trained on historical hiring data will perpetuate past gender or racial bias — it learns "who was hired" not "who should be hired".
📉
Representation Bias
Minority groups are under-represented in training data. Model performs poorly on these groups — e.g. facial recognition with 99% accuracy on white males but 65% on dark-skinned women.
🔗
Proxy Features
Even if gender/race are excluded, correlated features act as proxies: postcode correlates with race, name correlates with gender. Removing protected attributes is not enough.
📋
Measurement Bias
The label or feature measurement process is biased. Arrest rates (not crime rates) are used as a proxy for criminality — police patrol some areas more, creating a feedback loop.
🔄
Feedback Loops
Model output influences future data collection. Predictive policing sends police to predicted crime areas → more arrests there → model sees more crime there → feedback amplifies.
🎯
Evaluation Bias
Benchmarks don't represent deployment population. A model evaluated on benchmark X may be biased against groups under-represented in X but common in the real use case.

Fairness Metrics — Three Definitions

Demographic Parity: the model approves equal proportions across groups A=0 (e.g. female) and A=1 (e.g. male). Does not account for base rate differences. Can force inaccuracy.

The Impossibility Theorem

Chouldechova (2017) and Kleinberg et al. (2017) proved that most common fairness criteria are mutually incompatible when base rates differ across groups.

⚠️ You cannot simultaneously achieve Demographic Parity + Equal Opportunity + Calibration when positive rates differ between groups. Choosing a fairness criterion is a value judgement, not a technical decision.

Example: COMPAS Recidivism Tool

ProPublica showed COMPAS had higher false positive rates for Black defendants. Northpointe replied it was calibrated (equal predictive accuracy per score). Both were correct — they used different fairness definitions.

What to do?

  • Involve affected communities in metric choice
  • Audit across all fairness criteria; report tradeoffs
  • Consider whether the task should be automated at all
  • Document the chosen fairness definition and why

Regulatory Landscape

🇪🇺 EU AI Act (2024)

High-risk AI systems (credit, hiring, criminal justice, biometrics) must have human oversight, technical documentation, and provide meaningful explanations to affected persons.

🇪🇺 GDPR Art. 22

Right not to be subject to solely automated decisions with significant effects. Right to meaningful information about the logic involved (not a full technical explanation).

🇺🇸 Fair Credit Reporting Act

Adverse action notices must state specific reasons for credit denial. "The algorithm said so" is not compliant — must name actual factors like "too many inquiries".

🌍 Real World Example
Apple Card — When You Cannot Explain, You Cannot Defend
In 2019 Apple Card (Goldman Sachs) offered David Heinemeier Hansson a credit limit 20x higher than his wife despite identical assets. Goldman could not produce any explanation. The New York DFS launched an investigation; the case became a landmark regulatory argument: consequential models must be explainable by law. Goldman subsequently adopted SHAP-based audit reports for every credit decision.
